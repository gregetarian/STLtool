5. Which strategy should you pick?

Draft

Fast & cheap.

10% infill, 0.28 mm layers, 2 walls. Minimal plastic, visible layer lines, OK for prototypes and parts that don't bear load.

Pick when: throwaway prototype, fit-check, low cost matters more than appearance.

Standard

Balanced for everyday parts.

20% infill, 0.20 mm layers, 3 walls. Good surface finish, reasonable strength, sensible default for most jobs.

Pick when: general-purpose part, decoration, brackets, enclosures.

Solid

Strong, dense, slowest.

50% infill, 0.16 mm layers, 4 walls. Fine layer lines, much stronger interior, near-injection-moulded feel. The default for our quote tool.

Pick when: load-bearing part, customer-facing finish, end-use product.

6. How this works (plain English)

A 3D printer lays plastic down in thin horizontal layers. To quote a print, you need to know how much plastic and how much time. A real slicer (PrusaSlicer, Cura, Bambu) computes those numbers exactly by simulating every nozzle move — but slicers are big native programs that don't run in a web page. While we could do this in theory, we'd need to write or buy some software and have it set up to be permanently running on a server somewhere. This is doable, but difficult, expensive, and slow to run. So this tool approximates them in your browser to a reasonable degree using mesh geometry.

The shortcut, in 30 seconds

When the slicer prints a part, the plastic ends up in four kinds of place:

  • Walls (perimeter) — the outer skin of the part. We approximate this from the part's vertical surface area × wall thickness × wall count.
  • Top & bottom (skin) — solid roof and floor layers. We approximate from the part's footprint × skin thickness.
  • Infill — the sparse honeycomb that fills the inside. We compute the leftover internal volume × infill percentage.
  • Support — temporary scaffolding under overhangs. We cast rays downward from each overhang and measure the gap.

Add those up, multiply by a tiny calibration factor (1.06×) to account for how the slicer actually overlaps extrusion lines, and you get the predicted material in cm³.

Time is harder. The original heuristic time = material × constant was off by ~40%. The new model uses three terms — material, layer count (a tall thin part has many short layers each with their own overhead), and footprint (each top/bottom layer takes time proportional to area).

How accurate is it?

We sliced 147 real-world meshes of random 3D objects (from a free opensource dataset called Thingi10K) at three settings each in PrusaSlicer 2.9.4 to get 439 ground-truth measurements.

Held-out cross-validation:

  • Material: 5.7% median error on solid (the default), 7.1% on standard, 16.6% on draft. Overall 9.4%.
  • Time: 17% median error on solid. The 90th-percentile error is 48% — that's where the upper-bound on the quote range comes from.

A real slicer would hit ~2-5% but at the cost of a server, file uploads, and 5-30 second wait times. This tool's trade-off is honest: a slightly wider quote range, in your browser, in <2 seconds, with no infrastructure.

Why does the quote show a range?

Because the time prediction is on average 17% off, but in 10% of cases (long lattices, tall thin parts, anything that triggers cooling delays) it's 50%+ off. We measure that distribution empirically from the validation set and report the 10th-90th percentile as [low, high]. If you tell a customer "this will be £X.YZ" without a range, you'll occasionally lose money on the long-tail prints. The bracket is the honest version.

7. How the numbers are computed (for nerds)

Everything below runs in your browser. No file upload, no server, no database. The STL is parsed in JavaScript, the mesh metrics are computed in a single pass over the triangles, and the quote follows from a fitted physical model.

Mesh metrics

Volume

Signed-tetrahedron sum: for each triangle (v1, v2, v3) we compute V_tet = (v1 · (v2 × v3)) / 6 and sum over all triangles. The result is the enclosed volume regardless of where the origin sits — works for any closed mesh.

Vertical area, footprint, support potential

Each triangle's normal is classified by its z-component:

  • Vertical area = sum of areas where |n_z| < 0.7 (sidewalls). Becomes perimeter material.
  • Footprint = sum of areas where |n_z| > 0.7, divided by 2 (top-down silhouette). Becomes solid skin.
  • Support potential: for each downward-facing triangle (n_z < −0.7), cast a downward ray from its centroid; find the highest surface beneath using a 2D spatial grid + analytic ray-triangle intersection. Multiply triangle area by the gap, sum.

The raycaster correctly handles stacked geometry (a step in a pyramid sits directly on the step below, so the gap is zero — no phantom support).

Material model (deployed Model 4)

Material is decomposed into four physical contributions:

  • perimeter = vertical_area × wall_width × n_perimeters, capped at total volume
  • skin = footprint × skin_thickness, capped at remaining volume
  • infill = max(0, volume − perimeter − skin) × infill_fraction
  • support = support_potential × support_density

Total: material = scale × (perimeter + skin + infill) + support_density × support_potential.

Two global parameters fitted by NNLS against PrusaSlicer ground truth: scale = 1.0565 (slicer extrusion overlap correction) and support_density = 0.2919 (PrusaSlicer's sparse-support fill rate).

Validation (held-out 5-fold CV, 439 jobs from 147 real-world meshes via Thingi10K): 9.4% median, 35% p90 overall; per strategy solid 5.7%, standard 7.1%, draft 16.6%. Per-strategy refit was tested in notebook_v2.py (M1) and improved draft modestly but at the cost of solid; the deployed single global scale is kept because solid is the default and benefits most.

Print-time model (T3, new)

The original heuristic time = material × per_strategy_const was off by 37% median on solid. T3 replaces it with a three-term linear regression:

time = a_strategy × material + b_strategy × layer_count + c × footprint

where layer_count = bbox_z / strategy.layer_height and the third term captures per-layer skin-fill time. Coefficients fit by relative-loss NNLS:

  • a (h per cm³ of material): solid 0.0818, standard 0.0710, draft 0.0609
  • b (h per layer): solid 0.001061, standard 0.001123, draft 0.001288
  • c (h per cm² of footprint): 0.008993 (shared across strategies)

Validation (held-out 5-fold CV): 17.3% median, 48% p90 on solid. Compared to 42% median for the deployed material × const. A gradient-boosting regressor on the same features hit 18.2%, confirming we are at the linear-model noise floor for available features.

Low–high range (quantile band)

The point estimate for time is roughly 17% off in the median case. Rather than pretend it's exact, we show a low–high range derived from empirical CV residual quantiles:

  • P10–P90 band ≈ [0.86 × point, 1.80 × point]. 80% of validation jobs fall in this range.
  • Asymmetric on the high side: long-tail prints (lattices, tall thin parts with cooling delays) skew above the point estimate, so the upper edge is ~80% above the point but the lower is only 14% below.

For quoting customers, use the high-end of the range; for scheduling your queue, plan against the high end too. The point estimate is closer to the median print, not the worst case.

Cost roll-up

Final price per strategy is:

cost = setup + (weight × £/kg) + (time × machine £/h) + (time × power_kW × £/kWh) then × (1 + markup%)

Weight = material × density. The recommended (★) row is the cheapest after markup. Energy cost is usually small (£0.05–0.30 per print) but worth surfacing for transparency on long jobs.

Caveats and known failure modes
  • Non-watertight STLs generate phantom support potential (interior surfaces look like overhangs to the raycaster). One Thingi10K mesh had support_potential 6× the part's actual material — predictions on such meshes can be off by 100%+. Best practice: ask customers for clean, watertight files.
  • Tiny parts (<100 mm³) have median errors above 50% because per-print fixed overhead (skirt, brim, prime tower) is invisible to the model. Refuse to quote or apply a flat minimum.
  • Stepped geometry (e.g. layered terrain) under-predicts skin material because internal horizontal ledges are missed. Real slicers detect these via projection up/down by n_solid_layers × layer_height; we don't.
  • Time depends on printer: speeds, accelerations, fan curves all matter. The validated time model assumes a Prusa MK3-class printer with default profile. Faster printers will under-shoot, slower ones over-shoot.
Coefficients are editable above

Every parameter exposed in the UI is live: nozzle, skin thickness, infill, walls, layer height, machine rate, power, electricity, markup. The scale/support_density coefficients are baked into the JS for now — edit them in the source if you want to recalibrate against your own slicer/printer.
